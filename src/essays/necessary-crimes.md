Every functioning civilisation runs on small crimes.

Not the parasitic kind — not embezzlement, not graft for personal enrichment. The *other* kind. The fixer who knows which form to file and which to quietly lose. The building inspector who understands that the code violation in the heritage pub is keeping the neighbourhood together. The nurse who charts the medication at the time the system demands rather than the time the patient needed it.

These aren't bugs. They're load-bearing walls.

## The Operating System Analogy

Think of formal institutions as an operating system. Laws, regulations, procedures — they're the kernel. They need to be consistent, auditable, fair. But no operating system runs every process through the kernel. You'd grind to a halt.

Informal networks are userspace. The guy who knows a guy. The handshake that bypasses three months of procurement. The quiet agreement between neighbours that the council doesn't need to hear about. These aren't failures of governance — they're what makes governance survivable.

The formal system generates friction by design. It *has* to — consistency is expensive. Informal networks dissipate that friction at the cost of some corruption, with the net benefit of things being able to Actually Get Done.

## What Happens When You Kill Them

Algorithmic governance is the project of eliminating all necessary crimes. No discretion, no mercy, no common sense. Every interaction logged, every rule enforced to the letter.

You can see the results wherever it's been tried. Rigid bureaucracies seize up. Zero-tolerance policing criminalises poverty. Fully automated content moderation kills communities. The system becomes technically correct and functionally useless.

Imperial China understood this[^china] — formal courts existed, but most disputes were settled by family councils, guild mediators, village elders. People who could weigh context, apply judgement, and reach outcomes the law couldn't. The Romani *kris* courts operate the same way[^kris]: a parallel legal system that works *because* it's outside state authority, not despite it.

[^china]: See Philip Huang's *Civil Justice in China* for the definitive account of how Qing dynasty courts operated alongside — and in practice deferred to — community mediation. The formal/informal ratio was roughly 1:10.

[^kris]: The *kris romani* is a dispute resolution tribunal convened by senior community members. It has no state backing, no enforcement mechanism beyond social consequences, and yet it works — because the community recognises its authority as legitimate in ways the state system isn't. Walter Weyrauch's *Gypsy Law* covers this well.

When you strip these out, you don't get a cleaner system. You get a brittle one.

## The Distinction That Matters

Not all corruption is necessary, obviously. The distinction is structural:

**Load-bearing corruption** reduces friction, is roughly proportional to the problem it solves, and is distributed across many small actors. The fixer charges a fee. The favour gets returned. The system moves.

**Parasitic corruption** extracts value, scales with power rather than need, and concentrates in few hands. Regulatory capture. State-level kleptocracy. The revolving door between regulator and regulated.

The tragedy is that efforts to eliminate parasitic corruption almost always kill the load-bearing kind first. The powerful can afford lawyers and lobbyists to maintain their extraction. The neighbourhood fixer just stops fixing.

## The Uncomfortable Implication

If necessary crimes are genuinely necessary, then "zero corruption" isn't a policy goal — it's a civilisational threat. The question isn't whether to tolerate informal systems, but how to keep them load-bearing rather than parasitic.

This is not a comfortable position. It means accepting that the gap between law-as-written and law-as-practiced isn't a problem to be solved but a feature to be maintained. It means the anticorruption crusader and the algorithmic governance enthusiast might be making things worse, not better.

It means some crimes are more necessary than the laws they break.
